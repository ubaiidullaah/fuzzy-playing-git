History of the Internet

From Cold War research networks to the global web that connects billions of people today.

Origins ARPANET Protocols World Wide Web Browser Wars Web 2.0 Mobile Era Modern Internet

The Origins

1957

Sputnik and the Birth of ARPA

The Soviet Union's launch of Sputnik prompted the United States to create the Advanced Research Projects Agency (ARPA) within the Department of Defense, aiming to ensure American technological leadership.

President Eisenhower
1961

Packet Switching Theory

Leonard Kleinrock at MIT published his theory on packet switching, proposing that data could be broken into packets and sent independently across a network rather than requiring a dedicated circuit.

Leonard Kleinrock
1962

Galactic Network Concept

J.C.R. Licklider of MIT envisioned a "Galactic Network" of interconnected computers through which anyone could access data and programs from any site. He became the first head of ARPA's computer research program.

J.C.R. Licklider

ARPANET Era

1969

ARPANET Goes Live

The first message was sent over ARPANET from UCLA to Stanford Research Institute on October 29, 1969. The intended message was "LOGIN" but the system crashed after "LO." The network initially connected four universities.

Charley Kline & Bill Duvall
1971

First Email

Ray Tomlinson sent the first network email and introduced the "@" symbol to separate user names from computer names. Email quickly became the most popular application on the early internet.

Ray Tomlinson
1973

International Connections

ARPANET made its first international connections to the University College of London (England) and the Royal Radar Establishment (Norway), marking the beginning of a global network.

Protocols & Standards

1974

TCP Specification Published

Vint Cerf and Bob Kahn published "A Protocol for Packet Network Intercommunication," defining the Transmission Control Program (TCP) that would become the foundation of internet communication.

Vint Cerf & Bob Kahn
1978

TCP/IP Split

TCP was split into TCP and IP (Internet Protocol), creating the TCP/IP protocol suite. This layered approach allowed different networks to interconnect regardless of their underlying technology.

1983

ARPANET Adopts TCP/IP

On January 1, 1983 ("Flag Day"), ARPANET officially switched from NCP to TCP/IP. The Domain Name System (DNS) was also introduced this year, replacing numeric addresses with human-readable names.

Paul Mockapetris (DNS)
1986

NSFNET Backbone

The National Science Foundation created NSFNET, connecting five supercomputer centers at 56 Kbps. It rapidly expanded and effectively became the new backbone of the internet, opening access beyond military and academic use.

The World Wide Web

1989

Tim Berners-Lee Proposes the Web

Working at CERN, Tim Berners-Lee proposed a hypertext system for sharing information among researchers. His proposal outlined three fundamental technologies: HTML, URI (URL), and HTTP.

Tim Berners-Lee
1990

First Web Page and Browser

Berners-Lee created the first web server, the first web browser (called WorldWideWeb), and the first web page, which described the project itself. The foundation of the modern web was born.

1991

The Web Goes Public

On August 6, 1991, the World Wide Web became publicly available. Berners-Lee posted a summary of the project on the alt.hypertext newsgroup, inviting collaboration from around the world.

1993

Mosaic Browser Launches

The Mosaic web browser, developed at the National Center for Supercomputing Applications (NCSA), brought the web to a wider audience with its graphical interface and ability to display images alongside text.

Marc Andreessen

The Browser Wars & Dot-Com Era

1994

Netscape Navigator

Marc Andreessen co-founded Netscape Communications and released Netscape Navigator, which quickly dominated the browser market. The same year, the World Wide Web Consortium (W3C) was founded to develop web standards.

Marc Andreessen & Jim Clark
1995

Commercialization Explodes

The internet was transformed by a wave of commercial activity. Amazon and eBay launched. Microsoft released Internet Explorer, sparking the "browser wars." JavaScript was created in just 10 days at Netscape.

Brendan Eich (JavaScript)
1998

Google is Founded

Larry Page and Sergey Brin, two Stanford PhD students, founded Google based on their PageRank algorithm. Its clean interface and superior search results would make it the dominant gateway to the web.

Larry Page & Sergey Brin
2000

Dot-Com Bubble Bursts

The NASDAQ peaked in March 2000 and then collapsed. Hundreds of internet startups went bankrupt. However, the crash cleared the way for more sustainable companies and business models to emerge.

Web 2.0 & Social Media

2001

Wikipedia Launches

Jimmy Wales and Larry Sanger launched Wikipedia, a free online encyclopedia that anyone could edit. It became one of the defining examples of collaborative, user-generated content on the web.

Jimmy Wales & Larry Sanger
2004

Facebook and the Social Web

Mark Zuckerberg launched Facebook from his Harvard dorm room. Combined with the rise of blogs, RSS feeds, and platforms like Flickr, the web shifted from static pages to dynamic, user-driven content—the era known as "Web 2.0."

Mark Zuckerberg
2005

YouTube and Online Video

YouTube was founded and quickly became the go-to platform for sharing video online. Its "Broadcast Yourself" slogan captured the spirit of the era. Google acquired it in 2006 for $1.65 billion.

Chad Hurley, Steve Chen & Jawed Karim
2006

Twitter and Microblogging

Twitter launched, introducing the concept of microblogging with 140-character messages. It became a real-time communication platform that influenced news, politics, and culture worldwide.

Jack Dorsey

The Mobile Revolution

2007

The iPhone Changes Everything

Apple launched the iPhone, combining a phone, music player, and internet communicator into one device. Its touch-screen interface and mobile Safari browser redefined how people accessed the internet.

Steve Jobs
2008

App Stores and Android

Apple launched the App Store, and Google released Android. These platforms created an entirely new software ecosystem. Mobile apps became the primary way many people interacted with online services.

2010

Instagram and the Visual Web

Instagram launched, emphasizing photo sharing with filters. It symbolized the shift toward visual, mobile-first internet experiences. Within two years, Facebook acquired it for $1 billion.

Kevin Systrom & Mike Krieger
2013

Streaming Takes Over

Netflix began producing original content with "House of Cards," signaling the shift from traditional TV to internet streaming. Spotify, launched in 2008, was also transforming how people consumed music.

The Modern Internet

2016

AI Assistants Go Mainstream

Voice-activated AI assistants like Amazon Alexa and Google Assistant entered millions of homes, making the internet accessible without a screen. The Internet of Things (IoT) began connecting everyday devices.

2020

The Pandemic Internet

COVID-19 lockdowns made the internet essential for daily life. Remote work, online education, telehealth, and video conferencing (Zoom) surged. Global internet traffic increased by roughly 40% in a single year.

2022

Generative AI Emerges

The release of large language models and generative AI tools marked a new era for the internet. These systems could produce text, images, and code, raising profound questions about creativity, information, and the future of the web.

Today

5+ Billion Users Online

Over 5 billion people now use the internet—more than 60% of the global population. From a four-node research network in 1969, the internet has become the most transformative technology in human history.
